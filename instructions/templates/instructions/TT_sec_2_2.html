After having outlined what biases are, I would like to explain their causes in more detail. In simple terms you can think of two interrelated systems being involved in any decision-making process. System 1 runs fast, automatically, almost effortlessly and emotional. On the contrary, System 2 is slow, conscious, strained and deductive.
System 1 continuously generates intuitive suggestions for decisions, which are monitored and – if necessary – modified by System 2. Only System 2 can follow rules, evaluate alternatives and make well-considered decisions. Accordingly, if the decision-making process is associated with higher cognitive effort, System 2 will be activated from its generally relaxed control mode.
Why do I not just use this reflective system all the time, you may wonder. The answer is simple: As already illustrated by the driving example - you would simply be exhausted too fast. Therefore, it is efficient to let System 1 handle routine tasks.
For instance, have a look at the following equation: 2+5. Quickly and almost without questioning it, you will come up with the correct answer 7. System 1‘s intuitive suggestion will be accepted directly. If you now look at the other equation 34*27, you will notice that you have to put much more effort into finding the right solution with 918. Without suspecting it, you activate System 2 to solve the more complex equation.
However, in reality it is often the case that System 2 is not always automatically activated when more mental effort is required. Instead, System 1 often prompts wrong suggestions, especially when overloaded, and System 2 subsequently fails to correct them. This leads to cognitive biases in the decision-making process. Over the years, research has found several different cognitive biases that all have one thing in common: System 2 fails to overcome the intuitive suggestions of System 1.
